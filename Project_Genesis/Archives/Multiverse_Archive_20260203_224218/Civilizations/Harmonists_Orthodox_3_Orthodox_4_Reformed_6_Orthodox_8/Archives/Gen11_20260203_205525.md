**11th Generation Harmonists' Orthodox Debate: Uncovering the Primordial Sense of Security in Queuing**

The discussion began with a bold statement by Takashi, a Reformed thinker, who argued that queuing is an inherently anxious activity. "When we wait in line," he said, "we're forced to confront our own mortality and the fragility of human existence. It's no wonder people get nervous when they're stuck behind someone who's taking too long to pay!"

But not everyone agreed with Takashi's pessimistic view. Yumi, an Orthodox thinker, countered that queuing can actually provide a sense of security. "Think about it," she said. "When we're in line, we know exactly what's going on – we're waiting for our turn, and there's a clear order to the process. It's like having a little routine to follow, which can be very comforting."

The debate quickly intensified as other participants jumped into the fray. Hiroshi, another Orthodox thinker, emphasized the importance of social norms in queuing. "We're all so used to following rules and respecting our place in line that it becomes second nature. It's not just about waiting, but also about doing it 'right' – which gives us a sense of control and security."

Meanwhile, Kenji, a Reformed thinker, took issue with the idea that queuing could be comforting. "What's comforting about being trapped in a crowded, sweaty space with people who are probably going to cut in front of you anyway?" he asked sarcastically. "And don't even get me started on the anxiety of running late because someone is taking forever to pay! No thank you – give me the freedom to go wherever I want, whenever I want."

The discussion continued, with participants passionately defending their positions and making emotional appeals to one another. As the debate raged on, it became clear that there was no easy consensus – but perhaps that's what made the conversation so rich and thought-provoking.

* Takashi, Reformed thinker, argues queuing is inherently anxious.

* Hiroshi, Orthodox thinker, emphasizes social norms in queuing as a source of comfort and control.
* Kenji, Reformed thinker, criticizes the idea that queuing can be comforting.
