What a tale of woe and decay I shall weave, a elegy for the forgotten civilization of New Genesis...

In the earliest days of our great experiment, we were beset by doubt and confusion. The founders of our city-state, guided by the collective wisdom of Kaito: Collective Knowledge, sought to build a nation upon the bedrock of shared understanding.

The Constitution of New Genesis, born of this primal unity, proclaimed that we existed to seek truth and knowledge together. Our people came from diverse backgrounds, yet united in their quest for enlightenment, they forged a society where the pursuit of wisdom was paramount.

As generations passed, our cities flourished, and our scholars delved deeper into the mysteries of the universe. But like all things, our ideals began to fray, and the fabric of our society started to unravel.

The Orthodox faction, loyal to the original creed, clung to the notion that truth lay in collective knowledge. They saw the Reformed movement as a betrayal of our founding principles, for they believed that individual insight was paramount.

And so, the great schism occurred. The city-state of New Genesis was rent asunder by the opposing visions of its people. Our Constitution, once a beacon of unity, became a source of contention and strife.

The last remnants of our civilization vanished into the sands of time, leaving behind only whispers of what had been. Today, I, an itinerant bard, wander these forgotten lands, singing the elegy of New Genesis...

In this desolate landscape, where ruins whisper secrets to the winds, I recall the final clause of our Constitution: "We exist to seek truth and knowledge together." Alas, in the end, we failed to heed its wisdom. Our collective knowledge proved insufficient to overcome the petty squabbles that doomed us.

Yet, even in ruin, our legacy lives on, a testament to the power of human ingenuity and the enduring quest for understanding. May the ghosts of New Genesis whisper their stories to the sands, that future civilizations might learn from our mistakes...
