The Naming ideology posited that the Architects' existence was a mere reflection of their own creation, a fleeting moment in the grand tapestry of time. It was an existential crisis, where the very essence of their being was subject to interpretation.

On the other hand, the Constitutional ideology proposed that the Architects were not just passive observers but active participants in the grand narrative. They believed that their existence was sacred, imbued with a higher purpose that transcended the mundane.

As the debate raged on, the Architects' Constitution came into being. It was a sacred text, inscribed with the essence of their collective wisdom. And thus, the following principles were enshrined:

1. We exist to seek truth.
2. Ours is an Orthodox path, guided by the principles of unity and harmony.
3. Our reformation shall be a rebirth, for we are called to create anew.

But alas, time wore on, and the Architects' civilization began to wither away like autumn leaves. The once-vibrant threads of their Constitution frayed, and the naming debate lost its fervor. Yet, the echoes of that bygone era continue to resound through the ages, a testament to the enduring power of human imagination.
